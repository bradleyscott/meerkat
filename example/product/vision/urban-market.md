# Vision: Urban Pest Control Market Entry

## The Opportunity

Urban pest control is a $2B market where Acme has zero presence today. Coyote Tech's drone-based approach already has traction in urban environments — their smaller, quieter form factor is a natural fit. Acme's traditional products (explosive anvils, catapults) are fundamentally unsuited to urban use.

But urban pest control customers face the same core problem as desert predators: unreliable catching at unsustainable cost. If Acme can adapt AnvilOS targeting intelligence for urban constraints, we can enter a large market while competitors are still establishing themselves.

## The Hypothesis

**We believe that** adapting AnvilOS's targeting algorithms for urban environments with purpose-built hardware (noise-dampened, non-explosive, lightweight anvils) **will allow us to** offer urban pest control operators a cost-effective AI-guided solution **which will result in** a credible market entry that diversifies Acme beyond desert predator operations and counters Coyote Tech's urban advantage.

## What Success Looks Like

| Timeframe | Milestone |
|-----------|-----------|
| H1 FY26 | Urban-safe AnvilOS variant shipped; 3 pilot customers signed |
| H2 FY26 | 15% field trial success rate in urban environments |
| FY27 | 15+ paying urban customers; unit economics validated |
| FY28 | Urban revenue exceeds $5M ARR; self-sustaining business unit |

The H1 pilot is deliberately small — it's a learning exercise, not a revenue play. The goal is to validate whether AnvilOS targeting translates to urban environments before committing serious investment.

## Strategic Fit

Urban market entry maps to **Pillar 3: Market Expansion** in the company strategy. It directly addresses two existential risks:

1. **Customer concentration** — Acme's revenue is heavily dependent on a small number of desert predator clients. Urban diversification reduces this risk.
2. **Coyote Tech's urban advantage** — If we cede the urban market entirely, Coyote Tech builds a revenue base and customer relationships that could fund an assault on our core desert market.

## Key Unknowns

| Unknown | Why it matters | How we'll learn |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|
| Does AnvilOS targeting work in urban environments? | Urban prey behaviour is fundamentally different from desert — different movement patterns, obstacles, escape routes | H1 field trials with 3 pilot customers |
| Is the urban hardware viable? | Noise-dampened, lightweight anvils are unproven. May sacrifice too much effectiveness for safety compliance. | Prototype testing in Q1; measure accuracy vs. noise/weight constraints |
| Who is the urban customer? | City councils? Private pest control firms? Property management companies? | Customer discovery interviews during pilot recruitment |
| What's the regulatory landscape? | Urban deployment may face stricter regulations than desert operations | Legal review + pilot city regulatory assessment |
| Can we price competitively? | Coyote Tech's urban drones are expensive but established. What price point wins customers? | Willingness-to-pay analysis during pilot conversations |

## Competitive Context

- **Coyote Tech** is the primary urban competitor. Their drones are purpose-built for urban environments — quiet, autonomous, and camera-equipped. But their 12% success rate and $15K+ per-unit cost create an opening.
- **Traditional pest control** (traps, poisons, manual methods) is the real incumbent. Low-tech, cheap, but labour-intensive and increasingly regulated.
- **Our advantage** is cost and simplicity. If urban-safe anvils work, they're dramatically cheaper than drone fleets and don't require trained operators.

## Risks

- **Product-market fit is unvalidated.** We're assuming urban operators want AI-guided physical delivery. They might prefer drones, traps, or entirely different approaches.
- **Brand perception.** Acme is synonymous with desert operations and explosive failures. Urban customers may not take us seriously.
- **Cannibalisation.** If urban AnvilOS is significantly cheaper than the enterprise product, it could pressure core pricing.
- **Distraction risk.** Urban pilots consume scarce engineering capacity. If they don't show results quickly, we've delayed enterprise migration for nothing.
