# Roadrunner Systems

## Executive Summary

Roadrunner Systems is the market leader in prey evasion technology — the defensive counterpart to Acme's offensive solutions. Founded in 2005 in Boulder, CO, the company has grown to 800 employees and $300M in revenue by selling speed-enhancement, escape-route optimisation, and predator-detection systems to prey animals and their advocates.

The strategic concern for Acme is not Roadrunner's existing evasion business (which is complementary, not competitive), but their recent pivot into **"Guaranteed Catch Zones"** — controlled environments where predators are guaranteed a successful catch in exchange for a premium fee. If this model succeeds, it could disintermediate Acme's entire hardware and software platform by making the difficulty of catching irrelevant.

## Revenue Model

Two distinct business models:

**Legacy business (EscapeOS / PredatorAlert / SpeedBoost) — ~75% of revenue:**

- SaaS subscriptions for prey clients
- IoT sensor hardware + installation
- Managed services for wildlife preserves

**Guaranteed Catch Zones — ~25% of revenue and growing fast:**

- Per-catch premium pricing ($5,000–$25,000 depending on difficulty tier)
- Annual membership options ($200K–$1M for unlimited access)
- Catch Zone licensing to operators (franchise model in development)

## Competitive Landscape

Roadrunner Systems is unique in that they are not a traditional competitor — they come from the opposite side of the market. Their Catch Zones strategy represents a "platform shift" that could make the offensive solutions market (Acme's entire business) less relevant.

They view Acme and Coyote Tech as potential Catch Zone customers rather than competitors. Their strategy is to own the environment rather than the tools.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

### Strengths

- Largest environmental dataset in the industry (prey movement, terrain, weather)
- Profitable with strong PE backing — no funding risk
- 95% success rate in Catch Zones vs. industry average of 15-20% in open terrain
- Both sides of the market — sell to prey AND predators
- Brand credibility with regulators and conservation groups

### Weaknesses

- Catch Zones require massive physical infrastructure investment
- Limited to 15 locations — can't scale as fast as software
- Premium pricing limits addressable market to wealthy predators
- Cultural tension between legacy prey mission and new predator business
- Regulatory risk — animal welfare groups opposing Catch Zone model

## Strategic Assessment

### Threat Level: High

Roadrunner Systems represents the most significant strategic threat to Acme — not because they compete head-to-head today, but because their Guaranteed Catch Zones model could fundamentally change the market. If predators can pay a premium for guaranteed success rather than buying unreliable hardware for open-terrain hunting, the entire offensive solutions market contracts. The fact that Wile E. Coyote (Acme's flagship client) is now a Catch Zone member is an early warning signal.

### Where They Win

- **Success-rate-sensitive clients** — predators tired of failed attempts who are willing to pay premium for guaranteed outcomes
- **Regulated environments** — Catch Zones have regulatory approval; open-terrain hunting faces increasing OSHA and wildlife agency scrutiny
- **High-value captures** — when the cost of failure is high, the Catch Zone premium is justified
- **Clients frustrated with Acme reliability** — Wile E. Coyote's move to Catch Zones speaks volumes

### Where We Win

- **Open-terrain operations** — Catch Zones don't help predators who need to operate in the wild
- **Budget-conscious clients** — $5K–$25K per catch is 10-100x more expensive than Acme's per-drop pricing
- **Geographic flexibility** — we operate anywhere; they operate in 15 fixed locations
- **Autonomy** — some predators value the skill and independence of traditional hunting
- **Scale** — 200+ clients vs. 15 Catch Zone operators

### Positioning Implications

- **Do not dismiss the Catch Zone model** — acknowledge it serves a real need (guaranteed success)
- **Position Acme as "real-world solutions"** vs. "artificial environments"
- **Emphasise cost** — Catch Zones are luxury; Acme is practical and accessible
- **Highlight geographic freedom** — "hunt anywhere, anytime" vs. "book a slot at one of 15 locations"
- **Reliability roadmap matters more than ever** — our product failure rate is what drives clients to Catch Zones

### Roadmap Implications

- **Reliability is existential** — every product failure pushes a client closer to Catch Zones
- **Success-based pricing model** — consider offering "pay on catch" pricing to compete with the Catch Zone guarantee
- **AnvilOS intelligence** — invest in using our deployment data to increase success rates; close the gap with Catch Zones' 95%
- **Controlled environment offering?** — assess whether Acme should offer its own controlled hunting experiences (major strategic pivot)

### Key Watch Areas

- **Catch Zone franchise rollout** — if they successfully license to third-party operators, they could scale from 15 to 100+ locations rapidly
- **Wile E. Coyote behaviour** — is he reducing Acme spend, or using both? If he's shifting budget, other clients will follow
- **Regulatory decisions** — if regulators restrict open-terrain hunting but approve Catch Zones, the market shifts dramatically
- **Revenue mix tipping point** — when Catch Zones exceed 50% of revenue, expect aggressive expansion
